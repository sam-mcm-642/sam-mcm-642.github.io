My master's thesis tackles **referring expression comprehension** — given an
image and a phrase like *"the man in the red shirt to the left of the dog"*,
locate the exact object being referred to. Rather than learning this end to end
as a black box, I built a **neurosymbolic** system that separates perception
from reasoning: neural models read the image and the language, and a
differentiable logic engine reasons over the result to make the grounding
decision explicit and inspectable.

## Problem

Purely neural grounding models are accurate but opaque — they produce a box with
no account of *why*, and they struggle with the compositional, relational
structure of language ("left of", "holding", "the taller one"). Purely symbolic
systems can reason over that structure but are brittle: they break the moment the
words in the expression don't exactly match the labels a detector produced
("guy" vs. "man", "bike" vs. "bicycle"). The research question was whether a
**neurosymbolic** approach could keep the relational reasoning of logic while
using neural representations to absorb that vocabulary and perceptual noise.

## Approach

The system is a three-stage pipeline:

1. **Perception → scene graph.** A scene-graph generation model (a Motif-based
   architecture) turns the image into a structured graph of objects and their
   relationships — subject–predicate–object triplets grounded to image regions.
2. **Language → logical query.** The referring expression is parsed into a
   structured symbolic query (a set of typed constraints over entities and
   relations) using an LLM, so free-form text becomes something the reasoner can
   operate on.
3. **Reasoning → grounding.** A differentiable probabilistic-logic engine
   (**DeepSoftLog**, extending my supervisor's library) reasons over the scene
   graph to satisfy the query and ground it to the correct region. Its key
   property is **soft unification**: symbols match by *embedding similarity*
   rather than exact string equality, so "guy" can unify with a detected "man".
   That's what bridges the gap between neural perception and symbolic reasoning —
   and it yields a probabilistic proof, an interpretable trace of *why* a given
   object was chosen.

## My contribution

I formulated the problem, implemented the full pipeline, and ran the evaluation —
the scene-graph processing, the LLM query parser, and the integration with the
soft-logic reasoner, including extending the library to fit the grounding task.
Evaluation used IoU against ground-truth boxes plus accuracy and recall, with
analysis of how performance varied with the relational complexity of the query.

## Outcome

A working neurosymbolic grounding system that produces not just a predicted
region but an inspectable reasoning trace explaining it — demonstrating that
soft unification can connect noisy neural perception to structured logical
reasoning for visual grounding. The result is a research prototype rather than a
production system; the work is the method and the analysis of where relational,
interpretable reasoning helps.
